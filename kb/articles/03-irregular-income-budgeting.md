# Budgeting on Irregular Income, a Freelancer's Playbook

## Why salary budgeting rules break

Standard budgeting assumes a fixed number arrives on the 1st of every month. A freelancer or gig worker might receive Rs 95,000 one month and nothing the next, while rent, EMIs, GST payments, and insurance premiums stay fixed. The problem is not the average income, which may be healthy; it is the mismatch between lumpy inflows and steady outflows.

## Pay yourself a salary

The core fix is to separate earning from spending:

1. Route all client payments into one account that acts as the business account.
2. On a fixed date each month, transfer yourself a fixed "salary" into a personal spending account.
3. Set that salary at or below your lowest realistic earning month, or at 70 to 80 percent of your trailing 6-month average, whichever is lower.

Good months build a buffer in the income account; lean months draw it down. Your personal budget, rent, groceries, and SIPs all run on the fixed salary, untouched by the volatility.

## Size the buffers bigger

Two buffers matter, and both are larger than a salaried person's:

- **Income smoothing buffer**: 2 to 3 months of your self-paid salary sitting in the business account, absorbing the gap between invoices.
- **Emergency fund**: 12 months of essential expenses, versus 6 for salaried earners, because a downturn in your industry can shrink income for quarters at a time.

## Ring-fence taxes before spending

Freelance receipts arrive gross. GST collected belongs to the government, and income tax is not deducted at source the way salary TDS is, so advance tax falls due in four instalments through the year. A practical rule: move a fixed percentage of every receipt, commonly 20 to 30 percent depending on your slab and GST situation, into a separate tax account the day payment lands. Money in the tax account does not exist for budgeting purposes.

## Invest by rule, not by mood

Fixed monthly SIPs can be stressful when income is lumpy. Two workable patterns:

- Keep a small fixed SIP that even your worst month can cover, and top up manually in good months.
- Or run a rule such as: any month the buffer exceeds its target, sweep the excess into investments.

What does not work is investing only when you feel rich; that reliably buys high and skips low.

## The habits that matter most

Track expenses monthly like anyone else, keep personal and business spending in separate accounts for clean books and GST, and review the self-salary number twice a year. If you find the buffer growing every single month, raise the salary or the investing rate; if it keeps shrinking, cut the salary before cutting the tax transfers.
