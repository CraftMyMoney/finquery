# Nomination vs Will, What a Nominee Actually Is

## The common misunderstanding

Most people believe that naming a nominee on a bank account, mutual fund, or insurance policy decides who inherits that money. In most cases it does not. A **nominee is a caretaker, not an heir**: the institution's authorized point of contact who receives the asset and holds it in trust for the legal heirs.

Who the legal heirs are is decided by a **will**, or, when there is no will, by the succession law applicable to the person's religion (for example, the Hindu Succession Act or the Indian Succession Act). If the nominee and the legal heirs differ, the heirs' claim generally prevails, and the nominee is obligated to hand the assets over, a setup that has produced years-long family disputes.

## The exceptions worth knowing

- **Life insurance**: since a 2015 amendment to the Insurance Act, a "beneficial nominee" (spouse, children, parents) is entitled to the claim amount beneficially, not just as a trustee. Nomination here is closer to true inheritance.
- **EPF and similar**: nominations must generally be family members, and a valid family nomination carries strong effect.
- Joint holding with "either or survivor" passes operation of the account to the survivor, which is different from both nomination and inheritance.

The safe mental model remains: nomination ensures the asset is **released quickly** to a known person; the will decides who **owns** it. The two should agree.

## Why nominations still matter enormously

Without a nominee, releasing even small balances requires legal heir certificates, succession certificates, indemnity bonds, and months of paperwork, during exactly the period a grieving family is least equipped for it. Every account, folio, policy, and locker should have a current nomination, reviewed after marriages, births, and deaths. Unclaimed deposits in India run to thousands of crores largely because nominations were missing or stale.

## What a will does

A will is a written declaration of how your assets should be distributed after death. In India it needs no stamp paper and no mandatory registration (registration adds evidentiary strength but is optional). The basics: the person making it must be an adult of sound mind, it must be signed in the presence of two witnesses who also sign, and beneficiaries should not be the witnesses. A newer will supersedes older ones, so date it, and tell the executor where it is kept.

A will matters most when heirs are not the default line (wanting to provide for a dependent parent alongside a spouse, unequal splits, minor children needing a guardian) and when assets are scattered enough that the family may not even know they exist. A simple asset list attached to the will, accounts, folios, policies, properties, and liabilities, is half the document's practical value.
