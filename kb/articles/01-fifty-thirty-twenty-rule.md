# The 50/30/20 Rule and Needs vs Wants

## What the rule says

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple way to split your monthly take-home income into three buckets:

- **50% for needs**: expenses you cannot avoid without disrupting your life. Rent or home loan EMI, groceries, electricity and water, school fees, insurance premiums, medicines, commuting costs.
- **30% for wants**: spending that improves your lifestyle but could be cut in a pinch. Eating out, food delivery, OTT subscriptions, movies, shopping beyond basics, vacations.
- **20% for savings and debt repayment**: SIPs, PPF contributions, recurring deposits, emergency fund top-ups, and any loan prepayments beyond the minimum EMI.

On a take-home salary of Rs 1,00,000 per month, that means roughly Rs 50,000 for needs, Rs 30,000 for wants, and Rs 20,000 saved or invested.

## Telling needs from wants

The line between a need and a want is where most budgets fail. A useful test: if you stopped this expense tomorrow, would your health, work, or family obligations suffer? Groceries are a need; ordering dinner on an app is a want, even though both are food. A phone plan is a need for most working people; upgrading the handset every year is a want.

Some expenses split across both. A car EMI is partly a need if you commute by car, but the difference between a basic model and a premium one is a want.

## Adjusting the rule for Indian cities

In metro cities, rent alone can consume 30 to 40 percent of take-home pay, which pushes the needs bucket well past 50 percent. The rule is a starting point, not a law. If needs take 60 percent, trim the wants bucket first and protect the 20 percent savings line. The order of sacrifice matters: savings are cut last, not first.

A household with EMIs should count the EMI inside needs. If EMIs plus rent alone cross 50 percent of income, that is a signal of over-leverage worth addressing before any lifestyle spending review.

## Why 20 percent savings is the floor

Saving 20 percent of income consistently, invested at returns that beat inflation, is what compounding needs to build a meaningful corpus over a working career. Many planners suggest raising the savings share as income grows, since needs rarely grow as fast as salary. A common companion rule of thumb: your savings rate as a percentage should at least match your age.

## Putting it into practice

1. Compute last month's actual split from your bank statement before setting targets.
2. Automate the 20 percent: move it to investments on salary day, before spending starts.
3. Review the wants bucket monthly; it is the only bucket designed to flex.
