# Why Not to Mix Insurance and Investment

## The bundled products

ULIPs (unit-linked insurance plans), endowment plans, and money-back policies all combine a small life cover with an investment component. They are sold heavily because commissions are high, and they are bought readily because "protection plus returns" sounds efficient.

The arithmetic says otherwise: bundling usually delivers weak protection and weak returns in the same product.

## Weak protection

Bundled plans typically offer life cover of about 10 times the annual premium. A person paying Rs 50,000 a year into an endowment plan holds roughly Rs 5,00,000 of life cover. Compare that with the sizing rule for real protection, 8 to 10 times annual income plus debts: for someone earning Rs 12,00,000 a year, the endowment covers about 4 percent of the need. The family is effectively uninsured while believing otherwise.

## Weak returns

The premium in a bundled plan is eaten by charges before it is invested: premium allocation charges, policy administration charges, mortality charges, fund management fees, and surrender penalties. After all deductions, traditional endowment and money-back plans have historically returned around 4 to 6 percent a year, often below inflation. ULIPs improved after charge caps but still layer insurance costs on top of fund costs, and lock you in for five years.

For comparison, over long horizons, PPF has offered 7 to 8 percent tax-free with sovereign backing, and diversified equity funds have averaged higher still with market risk. The bundle underperforms both halves bought separately.

## The unbundled alternative

The standard advice is to separate the two jobs:

1. **Protection**: buy a plain term insurance plan sized at 8 to 10 times income plus debts. Cost for a young buyer: a small fraction of the bundled premium.
2. **Investment**: put the rest into instruments chosen for the goal, PPF and debt funds for stability, equity mutual funds through SIPs for long-term growth.

The same Rs 50,000 a year, split this way, typically buys 20 to 30 times more life cover and earns materially better long-run returns.

## If you already own one

Do not act in haste; exits have costs and the right move depends on how far the policy has run. General considerations people weigh: how many premiums are paid, the surrender value versus paid-up value, and whether the freed-up premium would actually be redirected to term cover plus investment. Buy the replacement term cover before discontinuing any policy that provides your only life insurance, never after.
