# Inflation and Compounding, Why Starting Early Matters

## Inflation: the silent debit

Inflation is the rate at which prices rise, which is the same as the rate at which money loses purchasing power. At 6 percent inflation, something costing Rs 100 today costs about Rs 179 in ten years, and Rs 1,00,000 kept in cash for ten years buys only what Rs 56,000 buys today.

This reframes "safe" options. A savings account paying 3 percent in a 6 percent inflation world is losing 3 percent of purchasing power a year with perfect reliability. The real return, return minus inflation, is what matters, and it can be negative in nominally positive products.

## Compounding: interest on interest

Compounding is growth applied to previous growth. Rs 1,00,000 at 12 percent grows by Rs 12,000 in year one, but by Rs 13,440 in year two, because the second year's 12 percent applies to Rs 1,12,000. Left alone for 25 years, that single Rs 1,00,000 becomes about Rs 17,00,000, and the final five years add more than the first fifteen did.

**The rule of 72** gives quick estimates: divide 72 by the annual return to get the doubling time. At 8 percent, money doubles in about 9 years; at 12 percent, in about 6. It works in reverse for inflation: at 6 percent inflation, prices double (money halves) in about 12 years.

## Why starting early beats investing more

Compounding's output depends far more on time than on the monthly amount. Two illustrations at 12 percent annual returns:

- Start a Rs 10,000 monthly SIP at age 25 and stop adding at 35 (Rs 12,00,000 invested, then left to grow): at 60, the corpus is larger than that of someone who starts the same SIP at 35 and contributes every year until 60 (Rs 30,00,000 invested).
- A one-year delay at the start, at age 25, costs more final corpus than a one-year gap at age 50, because the earliest rupees compound the longest.

The practical conclusion: a small SIP today beats a perfect plan next year. Increase the amount later; you cannot increase the time later.

## Compounding works against borrowers too

The same mathematics runs in reverse on debt. Credit card balances compounding at 3 to 4 percent monthly double in roughly two years untouched. Long loans front-load interest: in the early years of a 20-year home loan, most of each EMI is interest, which is why small prepayments early in a loan's life remove disproportionately large amounts of total interest.

The whole game of personal finance compresses to one line: get compounding working for you (start early, stay invested, beat inflation) and stop it from working against you (avoid revolving debt, prepay expensive loans early).
