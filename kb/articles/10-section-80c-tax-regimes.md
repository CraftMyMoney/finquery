# Section 80C and Old vs New Tax Regime, the Short Version

## What Section 80C is

Section 80C of the Income Tax Act lets you deduct up to **Rs 1,50,000 per financial year** from taxable income, under the old tax regime, for money put into specified instruments or spent on specified items. For someone in the 30 percent slab, a fully used 80C saves roughly Rs 46,800 a year including cess.

## What counts toward the Rs 1.5 lakh

Common qualifying items:

- Employee Provident Fund (EPF), your salary deduction counts automatically
- Public Provident Fund (PPF) contributions
- ELSS mutual fund investments
- Life insurance premiums (term or otherwise)
- 5-year tax-saver fixed deposits
- National Savings Certificate (NSC), Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana deposits
- Home loan principal repayment (not the interest; interest has its own section, 24b)
- Children's school tuition fees (tuition component only, up to two children)

Most salaried people already fill part of the limit through EPF and, if they have a home loan, principal repayment. Compute the existing total before buying new products in March; overshooting the cap earns no extra deduction.

## Old regime vs new regime

India runs two parallel personal tax regimes:

- **Old regime**: higher slab rates, but allows deductions and exemptions, 80C, 80D (health insurance premiums), HRA, home loan interest, NPS extras, and more.
- **New regime** (the default since FY 2023-24): lower slab rates and a higher effective tax-free threshold, but almost all deductions are gone. A standard deduction on salary remains, and the employer's NPS contribution stays deductible.

Salaried taxpayers can choose the regime each year at filing time.

## How to choose, conceptually

The comparison is mechanical: total up the deductions you would genuinely claim under the old regime (80C, 80D, HRA, home loan interest). If that total is large, typically for people with home loans, high rent in metros, and full 80C plus 80D usage, the old regime can still win. If deductions are small, the new regime's lower rates usually win, which is exactly the outcome the government designed it for.

Two cautions. First, do not buy poor products (bundled insurance is the classic case) purely to manufacture deductions; a bad investment is not redeemed by a tax break. Second, slab rates and regime rules change with Union Budgets nearly every year; verify current-year numbers on the official income tax portal before deciding, and treat any article's figures, including this one's, as a snapshot.
