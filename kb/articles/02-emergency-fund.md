# Emergency Fund, How Big and Where to Keep It

## What an emergency fund is for

An emergency fund is money set aside to absorb shocks: job loss, a medical event not covered by insurance, urgent travel, or a major repair. Its job is to keep you from breaking investments at a bad time or falling into high-cost debt like credit card revolving or personal loans when life goes wrong.

It is measured in months of expenses, not months of income. Add up your essential monthly outflow: rent or EMI, groceries, utilities, school fees, insurance premiums, minimum transport. That number times the target months is your fund size.

## How many months you need

The right size depends on how stable your income is:

- **3 months of expenses**: dual-income households where both earners have stable salaried jobs in different industries.
- **6 months of expenses**: single-income salaried households, which describes most Indian families with one primary earner.
- **12 months of expenses**: freelancers, consultants, gig workers, commission-based earners, and small business owners. Irregular income needs a bigger cushion because a lean quarter is a certainty, not a risk.

Example: a family spending Rs 60,000 per month on essentials with one salaried earner should target around Rs 3,60,000.

## Where to park it

The fund must be liquid and boring. The goal is availability in one or two days, not returns:

- Savings account or sweep-in fixed deposit for the first month or two of the fund.
- Fixed deposits or liquid mutual funds for the rest.

Do not keep the emergency fund in equity mutual funds, stocks, or long lock-in products like PPF. A market crash and a job loss often arrive together, and locked or fallen investments defeat the purpose. Gold jewellery does not count either; it is illiquid at fair prices and emotionally hard to sell.

## Building it when money is tight

Build it before starting aggressive investing. A common sequence: first one month of expenses, then insurance premiums covered, then grow the fund to target while starting small SIPs. Automating a fixed transfer on salary day works better than saving whatever remains at month end, because for most people nothing remains.

Refill the fund after every withdrawal, and revisit the target every year or after any big life change: a child, a home loan, a switch from salaried work to freelancing.
