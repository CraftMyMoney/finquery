# Credit Cards and the True Cost of Minimum Due

## The tool and the trap

Used one way, a credit card is a free 20 to 50 day loan every month with rewards on top: spend, receive the statement, pay the **total due** by the due date, pay zero interest. Used another way, the same card is among the most expensive debt legally available in India, at **36 to 48 percent a year** (3 to 4 percent a month).

The switch between the two modes is a single decision: paying the total due versus paying anything less.

## What "minimum amount due" actually does

The minimum due, typically 5 percent of the outstanding, keeps the card active and avoids a late fee. It does not avoid interest. The moment you pay less than the total due:

- Interest is charged on the entire outstanding, not the unpaid part alone.
- The interest-free period disappears: every **new** purchase accrues interest from day one until the full balance is cleared.
- Interest compounds monthly at 3 to 4 percent.

Example: an outstanding of Rs 1,00,000 at 3.5 percent monthly, paying only the minimum 5 percent each month, takes years to clear, and the interest paid along the way can approach or exceed the original amount. The same balance converted to a personal loan at 14 percent, or paid down aggressively from savings earning 6 percent, costs a fraction as much.

## Revolving debt is a red flag, not a strategy

Carrying a balance month to month is called revolving. Because card interest (36 to 48 percent) exceeds any realistic investment return, revolving while simultaneously holding investments is a guaranteed loss-making arbitrage: you earn 8 to 12 percent on one pocket while paying 40 percent from the other. With the exception of the emergency fund, paying off revolving balances is the highest-return "investment" available to anyone carrying them.

BNPL products (buy-now-pay-later apps and card-lite products) follow the same physics with smaller numbers: harmless if cleared on schedule, expensive fee-and-penalty machines when rolled over, and increasingly reported to credit bureaus either way.

## Using cards without getting used

1. Pay the **total due**, automatically, by auto-debit on the due date.
2. Treat the card as a payment method, not a credit line: if the money is not in the bank today, the purchase is debt, not spending.
3. Keep utilization under 30 percent of the limit for the sake of your credit score.
4. If a balance exists already: stop new spends on the card, and either convert to EMI at a lower rate or attack the balance before any investing beyond the emergency fund.
