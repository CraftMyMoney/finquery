# Retirement Corpus Rules of Thumb

## Why retirement needs its own math

Retirement is the one goal with no loan available and no second chance at compounding. It is also uniquely hostile to intuition: it is decades away, its cost inflates the whole time, and it must fund 25 to 30 years of living with no salary. Rules of thumb exist to convert this fog into checkable numbers.

## Milestones: income multiples by age

A widely used progress check asks how many multiples of your **annual income** you have saved for retirement (EPF, NPS, PPF, and investments earmarked for retirement, not the emergency fund or the house you live in):

| Age | Retirement savings target |
|---|---|
| 30 | 1x annual income |
| 40 | 3x annual income |
| 50 | 6x annual income |
| 60 | 8x or more |

Someone earning Rs 12,00,000 a year at 40 should have around Rs 36,00,000 marked for retirement. Being behind a milestone is common and fixable early, brutal to fix late: at 30 the gap closes with a modest SIP bump; at 50 it demands severe saving or postponed retirement.

## The end target: 25 to 30 times expenses

The corpus that retirement actually needs is expressed in **expenses, not income**: roughly **25 to 30 times your expected annual expenses at retirement**. The logic comes from sustainable withdrawal research: drawing about 3.5 to 4 percent of a diversified corpus annually, adjusted for inflation, has historically survived multi-decade retirements. Twenty-five times expenses is the inverse of a 4 percent draw; 30 times buys margin for India's higher inflation and longer joint life expectancies.

Note the compounding trap inside the estimate: it is future expenses that count. Household spending of Rs 50,000 a month today, at 6 percent inflation for 20 years, becomes about Rs 1,60,000 a month, pointing to a corpus of roughly Rs 5 to 6 crore, not the Rs 1.5 crore the today-number suggests.

## What these rules quietly assume

The multiples assume retirement around 60, several decades of equity-heavy compounding on the way there, and health costs handled by insurance rather than the corpus. Early retirement raises the multiple sharply (more years, fewer compounding years). A pension or rental income lowers it, since the corpus only needs to fund the gap between income and expenses.

## Using the rules without worshipping them

Rules of thumb are progress gauges, not plans. The workflow they support: check the age milestone yearly, estimate the end corpus from projected expenses, and set the SIP that connects the two, stepping it up with every increment. The single most reliable retirement lever remains starting the SIP a year earlier than feels necessary, since the earliest contributions do the heaviest lifting.
