# EMIs and the Debt-to-Income Ratio

## What DTI measures

Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is the share of monthly income that goes to loan repayments:

DTI = total monthly EMIs / monthly take-home income x 100

If you earn Rs 1,00,000 after tax and pay a Rs 22,500 home loan EMI plus a Rs 6,000 car loan EMI, your DTI is 28.5 percent.

## The benchmarks

Widely used thresholds in Indian lending and personal finance:

- **Up to 30 percent: comfortable.** Loans are serving planned goals without squeezing savings.
- **30 to 40 percent: caution zone.** Manageable, but one more EMI or an income dip creates stress. Most planners advise no new debt here.
- **Above 40 percent: over-leveraged.** Banks treat 40 to 50 percent as the ceiling when assessing new loans (lenders call their version FOIR, fixed obligation to income ratio). Above this line, savings usually stop and any shock lands on credit cards.

## Why the ceiling exists

Every rupee of EMI is a claim on future income that does not flex. Rent can be renegotiated by moving, wants can be cut, but an EMI missed damages your credit score and can escalate to penalties or repossession. When EMIs pass 40 percent of income, the remaining 60 percent must cover all needs, all wants, and all savings, which rarely works. The practical result is that the savings rate goes to zero, and the household becomes one emergency away from a debt spiral.

## Good debt, bad debt, and order of attack

Debt that builds an appreciating asset or earning capacity (a home loan, an education loan) sits at the healthier end, and usually carries lower interest because it is secured. Debt that funds consumption, personal loans at 14 to 24 percent and credit card revolving balances at 36 to 48 percent annualized, destroys wealth fastest.

If your DTI is high, the standard sequence is: stop adding new debt, list loans by interest rate, and prepay the highest-rate loan first while paying minimums on the rest (the avalanche method). Some prefer clearing the smallest balance first for momentum (the snowball method); the arithmetic favours avalanche, the psychology sometimes favours snowball.

## Keeping EMIs in check before borrowing

Before taking any loan, compute the post-loan DTI, not the current one. Include the new EMI, and stress-test it: would the ratio stay under 40 percent if income fell 20 percent? For a couple, run the test on one income if there is any chance of moving to a single income. The time to fix a DTI problem is before the loan is signed, because after signing, the only exits are prepayment or refinancing, and both cost money.
