# PPF, NPS and ELSS Compared

## Three tax-savers, three different animals

PPF, NPS, and ELSS all reduce taxable income under the old tax regime, which is why they get compared, but they differ in what they invest in, how long they lock your money, and how the proceeds are taxed. Choosing between them is really choosing a risk level and a liquidity level.

## Public Provident Fund (PPF)

- **What it is**: a government-backed small savings scheme; effectively sovereign-guaranteed debt.
- **Returns**: set quarterly by the government, historically in the 7 to 8 percent range, fully tax-free.
- **Lock-in**: 15 years, extendable in 5-year blocks. Partial withdrawals and loans become available after the early years.
- **Limits**: minimum Rs 500 and maximum Rs 1,50,000 per financial year.
- **Tax treatment**: EEE, exempt at contribution (80C), exempt growth, exempt withdrawal.
- **Role in a portfolio**: the safe, boring core of long-term debt allocation. The trade-off is the long lock-in and a return that only modestly beats inflation.

## National Pension System (NPS)

- **What it is**: a regulated retirement account investing in a mix of equity and debt through pension fund managers; you choose the mix or use an age-based auto option. Equity exposure is capped (up to 75 percent).
- **Returns**: market-linked; historically between debt and pure equity depending on the chosen mix.
- **Lock-in**: until age 60 for the main account. At exit, up to 60 percent can be withdrawn as a lump sum (tax-free) and at least 40 percent must buy an annuity, whose income is taxable.
- **Tax treatment**: contributions deductible under 80C, plus an exclusive extra Rs 50,000 deduction under 80CCD(1B) that no other instrument offers.
- **Role**: disciplined retirement building with the lowest fund management costs in the industry. The trade-offs are the hard lock-in and the mandatory annuity.

## Equity-Linked Savings Scheme (ELSS)

- **What it is**: a diversified equity mutual fund with a tax label.
- **Returns**: pure equity market returns; the highest long-run potential and the highest volatility of the three.
- **Lock-in**: 3 years, the shortest of any 80C instrument. Each SIP instalment locks for its own 3 years.
- **Tax treatment**: contribution deductible under 80C; gains at redemption are taxed as long-term capital gains on equity above the annual exemption threshold.
- **Role**: for investors who want their tax-saving rupee to also be their long-term equity rupee.

## Choosing between them

The practical questions: When do you need the money back? How much volatility can you hold through? Are you exhausting the extra NPS deduction? A common pattern is not either-or but layered: ELSS or equity funds for long-horizon growth, PPF as the debt anchor, and NPS for the extra 50,000 deduction if retirement lock-in is acceptable. Under the new tax regime most of these deductions do not apply, which changes the tax case but not the investment character of each product.
