# SIPs and Rupee Cost Averaging

## What a SIP is

A systematic investment plan (SIP) is a standing instruction to invest a fixed amount into a mutual fund at a fixed interval, usually monthly. A SIP is not a product and not a separate asset; it is a method of buying. Rs 5,000 a month into an equity index fund and Rs 5,000 a month into a liquid fund are both SIPs with completely different risk profiles.

## How rupee cost averaging works

Because the invested amount is fixed, a SIP automatically buys more units when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

Example over three months with a Rs 10,000 SIP:

| Month | NAV (Rs) | Units bought |
|---|---|---|
| Jan | 100 | 100.0 |
| Feb | 80 | 125.0 |
| Mar | 125 | 80.0 |

Total invested Rs 30,000 for 305 units, an average cost of Rs 98.4 per unit, below the simple average NAV of Rs 101.7. The dip did the buying for you. This is rupee cost averaging: it does not guarantee profits, but it removes the temptation to time the market and turns volatility from an enemy into a discount mechanism.

## Why the behaviour matters more than the math

The averaging benefit is real but modest. The larger benefit is behavioural: the SIP invests on schedule whether the news is euphoric or terrifying, which is exactly when manual investors stop. Long-term equity returns come disproportionately from staying invested through bad stretches, and an automated monthly purchase is the simplest commitment device available.

The common failure mode is pausing SIPs during market falls, which converts the mechanism's biggest advantage (buying cheap) into a loss. If the goal and the fund choice were right, a falling market is the SIP working as designed.

## Practical mechanics

- **Date**: pick a date just after salary credit, so investing happens before spending.
- **Step-up**: increase the SIP amount yearly with income, even 10 percent annual step-ups compound into a much larger corpus than a flat SIP.
- **Horizon**: match the fund to the goal. Equity SIPs suit goals 5 or more years away; for near-term goals, debt or liquid funds are the appropriate vehicle even though the SIP mechanics are identical.
- **Review**: judge a fund over years, not months, and compare against its benchmark, not against the best performer of the season.

## What a SIP does not do

It does not eliminate market risk; a 10-year equity SIP can still be down in a bad year. It does not make a poor fund good; averaging into a consistently underperforming fund just averages the underperformance. And daily or weekly SIPs offer no meaningful advantage over monthly; frequency past monthly is noise.
