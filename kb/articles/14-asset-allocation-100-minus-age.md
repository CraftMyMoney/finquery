# Asset Allocation and the 100 Minus Age Heuristic

## Allocation decides more than selection

Asset allocation is the split of your money across asset classes: equity (stocks, equity mutual funds), debt (PPF, EPF, bonds, debt funds, FDs), gold, and real estate. Decades of evidence say the split explains far more of a portfolio's long-run outcome and volatility than which particular fund or stock was picked inside each bucket. Most investors spend their energy on the opposite: agonizing over fund selection while their allocation happens by accident.

## The 100 minus age rule

A simple starting heuristic: **equity percentage = 100 minus your age**, with the rest in debt and other stable assets.

- At 25: roughly 75 percent equity, 25 percent debt.
- At 40: roughly 60 percent equity, 40 percent debt.
- At 60: roughly 40 percent equity, 60 percent debt.

The logic is horizon: a young investor has decades for equity volatility to resolve into growth and a salary to keep buying dips; an older investor has fewer earning years to recover from a crash and sooner needs to draw the money.

## A heuristic, not a law

The rule is a default to deviate from deliberately:

- **Adjust for the goal, not just age.** Money needed within 3 to 5 years does not belong in equity at any age; a 30-year-old saving for next year's wedding should hold that money in debt.
- **Adjust for temperament.** An allocation you abandon in the first crash is worse than a milder one you can hold. The right equity share is the highest one you will not panic-sell.
- **Count everything.** EPF and PPF are debt; many salaried Indians who feel "fully in equity" are actually 50 percent debt once EPF is counted. Real estate and gold holdings also belong in the picture.
- **Longer lifespans** push some planners toward 110 or 120 minus age; the principle of a glide toward safety matters more than the exact constant.

## Rebalancing: the discipline half

Markets move the allocation away from the target: after a big equity rally, a 60/40 portfolio may drift to 75/25, silently carrying more risk than chosen. Rebalancing means periodically selling the overweight asset and buying the underweight one to restore the target, typically once a year or when drift exceeds about 5 to 10 percentage points.

Mechanically, rebalancing forces the uncomfortable but correct action: booking some equity profits after rallies and buying equity after crashes. It converts volatility into a rule-driven buy-low, sell-high cycle without requiring any forecast.

## Diversification inside the buckets

Allocation across classes comes first, but spreading within each also matters: broad diversified equity funds rather than one sector or one stock, and gold capped at a small slice, commonly 5 to 10 percent, preferably through sovereign gold bonds or funds rather than jewellery, which loses making charges on both ends.
